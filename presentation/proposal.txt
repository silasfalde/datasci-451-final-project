Purpose
We aim to predict H1N1 and seasonal flu vaccination rates based on opinions, health behaviors, and demographics. We hope to strongly estimate the vaccination rate based on survey data and predict which groups or demographics are most/least likely to seek vaccination during a pandemic.
The Data
The data comes from the National 2009 H1N1 Flu Survey (NHFS), which was a “list-assisted random-digit-dialing telephone survey of households, designed to monitor influenza immunization coverage in the 2009-10 season.”

The NHFS was conducted between October 2009 and June 2010. It was a one-time survey designed specifically to monitor vaccination during the 2009-2010 flu season in response to the 2009 H1N1 pandemic. The CDC has other ongoing programs for annual phone surveys that continue to monitor seasonal flu vaccination.
The dataset contains about 26,707 observations and 36 feature variables made up of binary, ordinal, and categorical predictors. While the data was collected through a systematic random-dialing process, it is not a simple random sample. Instead, it follows a stratified cluster-sampling design with survey weights to better represent different geographic and demographic groups. Some weaknesses in the data include non-response bias from people who chose not to answer phone surveys, recall bias from self-reported vaccination behavior, and the fact that responses were not medically verified. If we were collecting the data ourselves, we would improve the design by integrating electronic health records to confirm vaccination status and by adding a web-based survey option to better capture younger populations. In our analysis, we will account for these collection methods by recognizing the role of survey weights during the exploratory phase and carefully handling the missing demographic values through structured imputation.
Development Plan
Our group of three has a clear division of responsibilities to make sure all grading requirements are covered. The first member will take the lead on the project setup and visual communication, focusing on the problem description, data cleaning, missing-value treatment, and data visualizations for the presentation. The second member will focus on model selection and computation, justifying methods such as Logistic Regression and Random Forest while making sure the models run efficiently and are tuned properly. The third member will focus on interpreting the results and improving code quality by turning the model output into meaningful public health insights while keeping all submitted code clean, well-commented, and easy to follow.
To stay on track, we plan to meet three times over the course of the project, either on Zoom or in person, to combine progress and align our work. The first meeting will focus on finalizing the data cleaning steps and creating the initial visualizations. The second meeting will be used to fit the first round of models, review performance, and make adjustments if needed. The final meeting will be dedicated to presentation practice and polishing the written report so it fully meets the ten-page academic requirement. We will use a shared digital workspace to keep one clean version of the project and clearly document each person’s contributions for the individual grading component. Our overall goal is to produce a report that combines strong model performance with clear interpretation so we can better understand the key factors that influenced vaccination behavior during the pandemic period. We haven’t done any preliminary data analysis other than simple data exploration.
